\section{Summary}
\begin{itemize}
\item \colorbox{green!20}{Green}: High efficiency (≥80\%)
\item \colorbox{yellow!20}{Yellow}: Moderate efficiency (50-80\%)
\item No color: Lower efficiency (<50\%)
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Key Observations:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Speedup generally increases with the number of jobs but shows diminishing returns
\item Larger images tend to achieve better parallel efficiency
\item Kernel size affects the computational workload and parallel scaling behavior
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Normalized Performance: Time per MAC}
\noindent This metric normalizes execution time by the number of multiply-accumulate operations (MACs = $H \times W \times 3 \times K^2$), providing insight into computational efficiency.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Time per MAC (nanoseconds) for CPU Parallel - Best n\_jobs}
\label{tab:cpu_time_per_mac}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Image Size} & \textbf{K=3} & \textbf{K=5} & \textbf{K=7} \\
\midrule
800×800 & 36.3474 & 13.5791 & 7.1607 \\
1600×1600 & 44.2241 & 16.2258 & 8.4222 \\
3200×3200 & 44.6305 & 16.1961 & 8.3102 \\
6400×6400 & 44.2959 & 16.3875 & 8.4040 \\
8000×8000 & 44.2434 & 16.0586 & 8.3288 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent \textbf{Interpretation:} Lower ns/MAC values indicate better computational efficiency. If time/MAC varies significantly with kernel size, it suggests the implementation may have different bottlenecks (e.g., cache effects, memory bandwidth) depending on $K$.
